Defending my series under 'fair use'.


This document is intended to justify why I believe that if legally pressed, my content could be successfully defended under fair use.

A precedent is a legal case that establishes a principle and/or ruling to be used in later disputes with similar issues. As of the time I am writing this, my series 'The Animation Warehouse' has never been cleared by any sort of legal authority. However, I believe that by going off of the principles set by previous successful defenses of fair use, there is a strong argument that it very well could. I aknowledge the fact that unless your work has been cleared by a qualified lawyer, you cannot and should not claim that it actually will fall under 'fair use'.

What is Fair Use?

'Fair use' is a limitation of a copyright holders' exclusive right to reproduce their own work. It is strictly prohibited to interpretative and/or transformative usages. While there is no criteria for what qualifies as a 'fair use', there have been many cases in which the defendant was able to successfully defend their work using fair use.

The following are the four factors judges use to resolve disputes of fair use, via Stanford's 'Copyright & Fair Use' Library. While helpful, even they are not set in stone and typically are adapted on a case-by-case basis. A more indepth explanation of each factor is available through the link provided.
  1. The purpose and character of your usage.
  2. The nature of the copyrighted work.
  3. The amount and substantiality of the portion taken, and
  4. The effect of the use upon the potential market.
"In particular, U.S. courts have recognized the following fair uses: criticism, comment, news reporting, teaching, scholarship, research and parodies."
— Electronic Frontier Foundation

Applicable Precedents Set By Successful Cases of Fair Use

Below are a list of usages allowed by the court that are applicable to my series. These have been identified by the Documentary Filmmakers’ Statement of Best Practices in Fair Use.
  • Commenting Critically on Media
  • Sampling popular culture to portray societal conditions
  • Incorporating incidental sounds and images in documentary footage
  • Employing archival material in historical or biographical sequences
A list of specific cases is availible at the Center for Media and Social Impact's page of 'Successful Fair Use in Documentary Films'.
